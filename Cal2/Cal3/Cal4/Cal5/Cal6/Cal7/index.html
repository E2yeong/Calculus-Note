Power Series, Taylor Series & Maclaurin Series

A Friendly and Complete Guide to Infinite Polynomial Expansions

1️⃣ The Big Question — Can a Function Be Built by Adding Infinite Pieces?

Usually, we think of functions like f(x) = x² or f(x) = sin(x). But what if we could create a function by adding infinitely many small polynomial pieces together?

This is the core idea of a Power Series.

2️⃣ The Basic Form of a Power Series

a₀ + a₁(x − c) + a₂(x − c)² + a₃(x − c)³ + ...

Or, more compactly:

Σ [ aₙ (x − c)ⁿ ], n = 0 → ∞
  • aₙ: coefficient of each term
  • c: center point of the series

So a Power Series is an infinite sum of powers of (x − c). If this series equals a function f(x), we can think of it as an “infinite polynomial version” of that function.

3️⃣ When Does the Series Work? (Convergence)

A Power Series doesn’t work for every x. It behaves well near the center c, but may blow up outside that range. We define the Radius of Convergence (R) to know where it works:

|x − c| < R → convergent  |x − c| > R → divergent

The Ratio Test is commonly used to find R.

4️⃣ Simple Example — The Geometric Series

1 + x + x² + x³ + ... = 1 / (1 − x), |x| < 1

Even though it adds infinitely many terms, the total is finite as long as |x| < 1. Once |x| ≥ 1, it diverges. This example captures the key idea of convergence: “Infinity can still produce a finite sum.”

5️⃣ From Power Series to Taylor Series

A Power Series is just an infinite polynomial, but what if we want it to exactly represent a specific function? Then we need to include the function’s derivatives — its shape, slope, and curvature — into the coefficients. That gives us the Taylor Series.

6️⃣ Taylor Series Formula

f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x − a) + [f''(a)/2!](x − a)² + [f'''(a)/3!](x − a)³ + ...
or
Σ [ f⁽ⁿ⁾(a) / n! * (x − a)ⁿ ], n = 0 → ∞
TermMeaning
f(a)Function’s value (height)
f'(a)(x − a)Slope correction (tangent)
[f''(a)/2!](x − a)²Curvature adjustment
[f'''(a)/3!](x − a)³Fine curvature change

Adding more terms makes the polynomial closer to the real function. The Taylor Series is therefore an infinite precision copy of a function near point a.

7️⃣ Why the “n!” Appears

Repeated differentiation multiplies by the power each time. For example, (x − a)³ differentiated three times gives 3 × 2 × 1 = 6. We divide by 3! to cancel that effect. So, n! corrects the repetition caused by derivatives.

8️⃣ Maclaurin Series — Special Case of Taylor Series

If the center is at a = 0, it becomes a Maclaurin Series:

f(x) = f(0) + f'(0)x + [f''(0)/2!]x² + [f'''(0)/3!]x³ + ...
or
Σ [ f⁽ⁿ⁾(0) / n! * xⁿ ]

This works beautifully for smooth functions like sin(x), cos(x), and eˣ.

9️⃣ Key Examples

(1) Exponential Function

eˣ = 1 + x + x²/2! + x³/3! + x⁴/4! + ...

Since every derivative of eˣ is itself, all coefficients are 1.

(2) Sine Function

sin(x) = x − x³/3! + x⁵/5! − x⁷/7! + ...

For small x, sin(x) ≈ x. The alternating signs ensure the wave shape.

(3) Cosine Function

cos(x) = 1 − x²/2! + x⁴/4! − x⁶/6! + ...

Starts at 1, then curves downward — matching its real graph near 0.

11️⃣ Radius of Convergence

Even though the series is infinite, it only behaves properly when the terms get small fast enough.

|x − a| < R → convergent  |x − a| > R → divergent

The value R defines the boundary of validity and can be found using the Ratio Test.

13️⃣ Final Thoughts

A Power Series is an “infinite polynomial world.” A Taylor Series fills that world with the actual behavior of a real function. The Maclaurin Series is its simplest form around zero. Ultimately, to fully understand a function is to understand its Taylor Series.
