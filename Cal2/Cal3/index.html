1) Hyperbolic Functions (pp. 261–269)

Why learn them?

Hyperbolic functions mirror many trigonometric identities but are defined via exponentials. They model the catenary (shape of a hanging chain), minimal surfaces, and appear in special relativity.

Definitions & parity

\(\sinh x\)
\(\displaystyle \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}\) (odd)
\(\cosh x\)
\(\displaystyle \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}\) (even)
\(\tanh x\)
\(\displaystyle \frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x}\in(-1,1)\) (odd)

Key identity (hyperbolic Pythagorean): \(\cosh^2 x - \sinh^2 x = 1\).

Derivatives & integrals

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\sinh x = \cosh x\), \(\;\; \int \cosh x\,dx = \sinh x + C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\cosh x = \sinh x\), \(\;\; \int \sinh x\,dx = \cosh x + C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\tanh x = \operatorname{sech}^2 x\)
  • \(\operatorname{sech} x = 1/\cosh x\), \(\operatorname{csch} x = 1/\sinh x\), \(\coth x = 1/\tanh x\)
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\,\operatorname{sech}x = -\operatorname{sech}x\,\tanh x\)
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\,\operatorname{csch}x = -\operatorname{csch}x\,\coth x\)

Inverse hyperbolics (useful forms)

  • \(\displaystyle \operatorname{arcsinh}\,x = \ln\!\big(x+\sqrt{x^2+1}\big)\), \(\;\; \frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{arcsinh}x=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}\)
  • \(\displaystyle \operatorname{arccosh}\,x = \ln\!\big(x+\sqrt{x^2-1}\big)\) (domain \(x\ge 1\)), \(\;\; \frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{arccosh}x=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2-1}}\)
  • \(\displaystyle \operatorname{arctanh}\,x = \tfrac12 \ln\!\frac{1+x}{1-x}\) (domain \(|x|<1\)), \(\;\; \frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{arctanh}x=\frac{1}{1-x^2}\)
Catenary: The curve \(y=a\cosh(x/a)\) solves the hanging-chain problem; unlike a parabola, its curvature matches physics of uniform density and gravity.

2) Extrema & Derivatives (pp. 280–289)

Critical points

  • \(c\) is a critical point if \(f'(c)=0\) or \(f'\) is undefined while \(f\) is defined.
  • Candidates for local extrema occur at critical points and endpoints (for closed intervals).

Second derivative test

  • If \(f'(c)=0\) and \(f''(c)>0\): local minimum; if \(f''(c)<0\): local maximum.
  • If \(f''(c)=0\): inconclusive — use first derivative sign chart or higher derivatives.

Example

\(f(x)=x^3-3x^2+2\)

  • \(f'(x)=3x^2-6x=3x(x-2)\Rightarrow x=0,2\).
  • \(f''(x)=6x-6\Rightarrow f''(0)=-6\) (local max at \(x=0\)); \(f''(2)=6\) (local min at \(x=2\)).
Endpoint awareness: On \([a,b]\), compare \(f\) at critical points and at \(a,b\) to find absolute extrema.

3) Mean Value Theorem (pp. 290–296)

Statement

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\), then \(\exists c\in(a,b)\) such that \[ f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \]

Special case (Rolle’s Theorem): if \(f(a)=f(b)\), then \(\exists c\) with \(f'(c)=0\).

Why it matters

  • Bridges average and instantaneous rate of change.
  • Proves monotonicity criteria and error bounds for approximations.
  • Real-world reading: if you drove 300 km in 3 h, then at some instant your speed was exactly 100 km/h.
Common pitfall: Check hypotheses: continuity on \([a,b]\) and differentiability on \((a,b)\) are required.

4) Graph Shape from \(f'\) and \(f''\) (pp. 296–309)

Sign analysis

  • \(f'>0\Rightarrow\) increasing; \(f'<0\Rightarrow\) decreasing.
  • \(f''>0\Rightarrow\) concave up; \(f''<0\Rightarrow\) concave down.
  • Inflection points: where concavity changes (often \(f''=0\) with sign change).

Workflow

  1. Domain & discontinuities.
  2. Intercepts and asymptotes.
  3. Critical points (from \(f'=0\) or undefined).
  4. Increasing/decreasing intervals (sign of \(f'\)).
  5. Concavity & inflection (sign of \(f''\)).
  6. Assemble a coherent sketch.
Heuristic: Use sign charts for \(f'\) and \(f''\). They tell a consistent “story” of the graph without plotting many points.

5) L’Hôpital’s Rule (pp. 309–319)

Purpose

Evaluates indeterminate forms \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty\) by differentiating numerator and denominator: \[ \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \quad \text{if the latter limit exists and } f,g \text{ are differentiable near } a. \]

Extensions via algebra

  • \(0\cdot\infty\): rewrite as \(\frac{f}{1/g}\) or \(\frac{g}{1/f}\).
  • \(\infty-\infty\): combine over common denominator.
  • \(0^0, 1^\infty, \infty^0\): take logs \(y=f^g \Rightarrow \ln y = g\ln f\), then exponentiate after limit.

Classic example

\(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x}\)

Direct \(0/0\). By L’Hôpital: \(\lim\frac{\cos x}{1}=1\).

Check differentiability and indeterminate form first. L’Hôpital does not apply to determinate limits, and repeated application without simplification can mislead.

6) Curve Sketching (pp. 320–336)

Exam-ready checklist

  1. Domain, intercepts, symmetries (even/odd/periodic).
  2. Asymptotes: vertical (poles/zeros of denominator), horizontal/oblique (end behavior).
  3. Critical points: solve \(f'=0\) or undefined; classify (1st/2nd derivative tests).
  4. Monotonicity: intervals of \(f'>0\) / \(f'<0\).
  5. Concavity & inflection: \(f''\) sign and changes.
  6. Assemble the sketch: mark features to scale qualitatively.
Tip: If rational, factor and cancel common terms first — removable discontinuities affect the sketch (holes).

7) Optimization Problems (pp. 336–351)

General modeling workflow

  1. Define variables and draw a diagram.
  2. Use constraints to express objective as a single-variable function \(F(x)\).
  3. Find critical points: solve \(F'(x)=0\) and check endpoints/feasible boundaries.
  4. Classify maxima/minima (2nd derivative or direct comparison).
  5. Interpret solution in the original context; check units and feasibility.

Classics

  • Fixed perimeter rectangle → max area is a square.
  • Fixed volume cylinder → min surface area (optimize radius/height).
  • Min distance from a point to a line/curve (objective: squared distance simplifies derivatives).
Model first, compute second: The hardest step is turning words into equations. Once \(F(x)\) is correct, calculus is routine.

8) Newton’s Method (pp. 351–356)

Iteration

\[ x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}. \]

Geometric idea: extend the tangent line at \((x_n,f(x_n))\) to the \(x\)-axis; its intercept is the next approximation.

Convergence behavior

  • Quadratic convergence near a simple root (fast improvement).
  • Can fail if \(f'(x_n)=0\) or if starting far from the root (divergence, cycles).
  • Multiple roots slow convergence to linear order.

Example: \(\sqrt{2}\)

Let \(f(x)=x^2-2\), \(f'(x)=2x\). With \(x_0=1\): \(x_1=1.5\), \(x_2\approx1.4167\), \(x_3\approx1.4142\).

Practical tips: choose a reasonable initial guess (graph or bisection first), set stopping criteria (e.g., \(|x_{n+1}-x_n|<\varepsilon\) or \(|f(x_n)|<\varepsilon\)).

9) Antiderivatives (p. 356)

Concept

An antiderivative \(F\) of \(f\) satisfies \(F'=f\). The general antiderivative is \(F(x)+C\) (family shifted by a constant).

Core table

  • \(\displaystyle \int k\,dx = kx + C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int x^n dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C\) \((n\neq -1)\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int e^x dx = e^x + C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int a^x dx = \frac{a^x}{\ln a}+C\) \((a>0,a\neq1)\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x+C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int \cos x\,dx = \sin x+C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int \sec^2 x\,dx = \tan x+C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int \csc^2 x\,dx = -\cot x+C\)
  • \(\displaystyle \int \sec x \tan x\,dx = \sec x+C\)

Methods

  • Substitution: \(u=g(x)\), \(du=g'(x)\,dx\) (reverse chain rule).
  • Integration by parts: \(\int u\,dv=uv-\int v\,du\) (LIATE heuristic).
  • Partial fractions: decompose rational functions (proper) into simpler terms.
  • Trig identities: use Pythagorean & power-reduction identities for \(\sin^m x\cos^n x\).
